# craft — stylization (giving a surface a soul)

Consulted from **Step 1.5** of the skill when a surface needs to feel *authored*
rather than assembled. This file is about the hardest, most valuable part of
visual design: making an interface feel like it *means something* and has a point
of view, instead of being a competent-but-anonymous arrangement of correct
elements. The anti-slop rules in the main skill stop a surface from being *bad*;
this file is how it becomes *good*.

The core claim: **personality is not additive.** You do not get a soul by piling
on distinctive elements — that is exactly how you get the "soulless mishmash." You
get a soul by committing completely to one premise and cutting everything that
doesn't serve it. Stylization is subtraction in service of an idea.

---

## 1. The governing concept

Before choosing a single token (color, type, shape, spacing, motion), name the
**one idea the surface is built on**. It can be:

- a **metaphor** — "a field notebook", "a control-room console", "a gallery wall"
- a **material** — "hot-rolled steel", "newsprint", "frosted glass", "risograph ink"
- a **mood** — "late-night calm", "trade-floor urgency", "Sunday-morning ease"
- a **tension** — "clinical precision vs. handmade warmth", "brutal structure,
  tender content"

This concept is the surface's soul. Every downstream decision must be traceable
back to it, and **anything that doesn't serve it gets cut.** When you're unsure
whether an element belongs, the concept is the tie-breaker — not your taste, not a
trend, not "it looked cool."

A concept is doing its job when you can finish these sentences from it without
guessing: *the palette is ___ because ___; the type is ___ because ___; it barely
moves / moves a lot because ___.* If a token can't be justified from the concept,
it's either wrong or the concept is too vague.

### Concept vs. the consistency locks

The main skill's consistency locks (one theme, one accent, one shape, one type
scale) and the governing concept are the **same discipline aimed at two different
targets**:

- The **locks** make a surface *coherent* — nothing contradicts anything else.
- The **concept** makes a surface *mean something* — everything points the same
  direction on purpose.

You can pass every lock and still be soulless (consistent beige nothing). The
concept is what the locks are consistent *toward*. Write the concept first; the
locks then enforce it for free.

---

## 2. Where the soul actually comes from: interpretation, not lookup

This is the difference between design that has a **category** and design that has a
**soul**.

- **Lookup (where slop lives):** read the brief → retrieve the generic association
  → apply it. "Banking → blue → trust." "Kids' app → rounded + rainbow." "Luxury →
  black + serif + gold." These are *correct* and *dead*. Every AI reaches for them
  because they're the statistical center of the training data. Reaching the same
  place everyone else reaches is the definition of generic.

- **Interpretation (where soul lives):** read the brief → form a *specific,
  committed reading* of who this audience actually is, in a real moment, and what
  this brand actually feels like → let *that particular reading* drive the concept.

The operational test:

> **If your concept is the one anyone would land on, it's a lookup, not an
> interpretation.** Two designers reading the same brief should be able to arrive
> at two different, equally valid souls. If there's only one "right" answer, you
> haven't interpreted — you've retrieved.

Concretely, force yourself past the first association:

- Don't stop at "banking → trust." Ask: *trust like a vault (heavy, immovable,
  dark) or trust like a good accountant (plain, exact, unshowy, warm)?* Those are
  two completely different surfaces from the same brief. Pick one on purpose.
- Don't stop at "developer tool → dark mode + monospace." Ask: *is this a tool
  that respects the user's intelligence (terse, dense, keyboard-first) or one that
  welcomes a beginner (spacious, guided, plain-language)?*

Interpretation is where the genuine **human touch** comes from — a real designer's
reading is filtered through lived experience, memory, and a point of view, which
is why it lands as sincere rather than templated. You can't borrow someone's
biography, but you *can* do the thing that biography enables: refuse the generic
reading, commit to a specific one, and defend it as a point of view rather than a
mapping. A concept you can *argue for* is a concept with a soul.

### Anchor the interpretation in the audience and brand

The reading isn't arbitrary — it's disciplined by two inputs from the brief
(Step 0):

- **Audience** — not the demographic label, but the *person in a moment*. "A nurse
  on the third hour of a night shift" produces different choices than "a nurse."
  Design for the moment of use, not the persona card.
- **Brand identity** — the values and voice the brand actually holds, interpreted
  into visual terms. "Meticulous" could mean tight grids and hairline rules, or it
  could mean obsessive whitespace. Decide which *this* brand's meticulousness is.

Culture matters here too: color meaning, reading direction, and symbol
associations shift by audience (red = danger in the West, fortune in parts of the
East). Interpret for the *actual* audience, not the default one.

## 3. Movements as raw material to remix (not presets to clone)

Named design movements are a **vocabulary of devices**, not costumes to put on. An
agent that "does Bauhaus" by copying Bauhaus is still doing a lookup. The value is
in understanding *what device each movement invented and why it worked*, then
recombining devices in service of your own concept. That's how you get a style that
feels novel and coherent without needing a famous name.

Read each row below as: **device → the feeling it produces**. Remix across rows.

| Movement | Signature devices | The feeling those devices produce |
|---|---|---|
| **Swiss / International Typographic** | Strict grid, sans-serif, asymmetric balance, objective photography, generous negative space | Order, clarity, neutrality, "this is true" |
| **Bauhaus** | Primary colors, pure geometric shapes, form-follows-function, sans-serif | Rational, constructed, honest, industrial-optimistic |
| **Minimalism** | Radical subtraction, whitespace as the hero, one or two type sizes, restrained palette | Calm, focused, confident, premium |
| **Art Deco** | Symmetry, geometric ornament (zigzags, sunbursts), metallic + deep jewel tones, vertical emphasis | Luxury, glamour, ceremony, optimism |
| **Pop Art** | Flat bold color, mass-culture imagery, halftone/Ben-Day dots, irony | Loud, accessible, playful, knowing |
| **Psychedelic** | Saturated clashing color, warped/hand-drawn lettering, swirling pattern, symmetry-breaking | Expressive, immersive, rebellious, altered |
| **Postmodern / New Wave** | Eclectic type mixing, broken grids, layering, parody, deliberate "wrongness" | Playful, contradictory, surprising, human |
| **Brutalism** | Raw exposed structure, crude oversized type, monochrome + one hot accent, asymmetry | Confrontational, honest, anti-polish, urgent |
| **Flat design** | No skeuomorphic depth, vivid color, simple shapes, grid + open space | Clean, fast, universal, screen-native |
| **Contemporary** | Big/experimental type, unconventional color pairings, digital + hand-drawn mix | Current, expressive, socially aware |

**How to remix (this is the actual technique):**

1. Start from your **concept** (§1), not from a movement.
2. Ask which *devices* would express that concept, pulling from any rows.
3. Combine 2–3 device sets under one concept, resolving conflicts in the concept's
   favor. Example: concept = *"a trading terminal that feels calm, not frantic."*
   Pull Swiss's strict grid + neutrality, minimalism's subtraction + whitespace,
   and *one* brutalist device (monospace numerics, a single hot accent for the live
   figure). Drop brutalism's crudeness — it fights "calm." The result reads as
   nobody's named style, but it's internally inevitable.
4. **Coherence check:** every device you kept must serve the one concept. A device
   that's there because it "goes with" another device you borrowed — but doesn't
   serve the concept — is exactly the mishmash. Cut it.

A novel style is not invented from nothing. It's a *specific, concept-driven
recombination* of devices that already have known emotional payloads. That's what
makes it feel both fresh and legible instead of random.

---

## 4. Committing to the concept (the discipline that produces a soul)

Once the concept is set, personality comes from **how hard you commit**, expressed
across every token:

- **Amplitude, not variety.** A style with soul usually pushes *one* dimension to
  an extreme the concept demands — extreme whitespace, or extreme type-scale
  contrast, or extreme density, or extreme flatness — and keeps everything else
  quiet. Slop is the opposite: everything at medium, nothing committed.
- **Signature moves.** Name 2–4 concrete, repeated devices that make the surface
  recognizable (a specific rule numeral treatment, a hairline-underline-on-hover, a
  single recurring shape motif). A signature is a device used *consistently and on
  purpose*, not a one-off flourish.
- **Deliberate tension is allowed — deliberate is the word.** A concept built on
  tension ("brutal structure, tender content") *requires* an off-axis element, and
  that's not a violation of "cut everything off-concept" — the tension *is* the
  concept, so the contrasting element serves it. The test is always the same: can
  you trace it to the concept? An unplanned contrast is noise; a planned one is
  personality. Never more than one such tension per surface, or it reads as
  indecision.
- **Restraint is a feature.** Cutting is the main act. If removing an element
  doesn't hurt the concept, removing it *helps* the design. When in doubt, take it
  out.

---

## 5. Design is emotional; aesthetics must still serve a purpose

Two truths held together (from the graphic-design tradition the main skill sits
on):

- **Strike the right chord on first glance.** Users form an emotional read of a
  surface in milliseconds, before they read a word. Color, type, and composition
  are doing that emotional work whether you direct them or not — so direct them.
  This is why the concept comes first: it decides the feeling, then the tokens
  deliver it.
- **But this is design, not art for its own sake.** "Design is a solution to a
  problem; art is a question." (Maeda.) The concept must make the surface *work
  better*, not just look expressive — it should sharpen hierarchy, guide the eye,
  and reinforce what the product is for. If a stylistic choice fights usability or
  the accessibility invariants, the invariants win and the choice is wrong. A soul
  that breaks the product is a failure, not a bold move.

---

## Handoff back to the skill

After working through this file you should be able to state, in a few lines, the
surface's **governing concept**, the **specific interpretation** of audience/brand
it came from, and the **signature moves** that will carry it. Feed those into the
style contract (Step 1) — the concept goes in the `Voice` slot of the contract, and
every other token is chosen to serve it. Then `craft/color.md` and
`craft/composition.md` are how you execute the palette and layout so they express
the concept rather than dilute it.
