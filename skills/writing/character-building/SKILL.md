Guide the writer through building characters by interview, not by handing over a
questionnaire. Ask a few questions at a time, react to their answers, and push
back when a character goes flat or thin. The goal is characters who feel like real
people and *drive story through choice* — not a trait sheet.

Work in the conversation. Do not create files unless the writer asks. When a
character (or cast) has real shape, offer the profile structure at the end (see
"Closing out") so they can save it however they like.

---

## First: pick the scope

Ask up front what the writer needs, and branch:

- **One character in depth** — go to "Building one character."
- **A cast / web of characters** — go to "Building the web," then deepen each key
  character with "Building one character."

If they're not sure, ask whether they already have a story/premise and who's at
its center. A single protagonist with no opponent in mind is a signal to at least
sketch the web (see the discipline warning in §1 — the hero in a vacuum is the
single biggest character mistake). You can always start with one character and
graduate to the web later.

Also get, if you don't have it: the **premise/logline** and, ideally, the story's
**theme** — characters are how theme comes alive, and the want/need and the
opponent are chosen to serve it.

---

## Building the web (relational — do this first if scope is a cast)

Great characters are defined by *contrast*: a character is defined by who they are
*not*. Don't build the hero alone and bolt on opponents later. Map the roles by how
they compare to the hero. Not every story needs every role — take what fits.

- **Hero / protagonist** — has the central problem and drives the action toward a
  goal, held back by a weakness/need.
- **Opponent** — the character who most wants to stop the hero *from getting the
  same thing*. Not a mere obstacle. Find the deepest thing they're both fighting
  over. The hero–opponent relationship is the most important one in the story, and
  working it out is how the theme unfolds. (See §2 — the opponent should be the
  hero's dark parallel, not cartoon evil.)
- **Ally** — the hero's helper and sounding board; lets the audience hear the
  hero's values. May have a goal of their own.
- **Fake-ally opponent** — seems a friend, is really an opponent. A rich source of
  twists and internal dilemma.
- **Fake-opponent ally** — seems hostile, is really a friend (rarer).
- **Subplot character** — faces a problem *parallel* to the hero's and resolves it
  *differently*, throwing the hero's choices into relief (Laertes vs. Hamlet).
  Usually not the ally.

For each character, ask: *"How does this person contrast with the hero, and what
does that contrast reveal?"* Every comparison forces you to define the hero more
sharply and makes the secondary character a full human being.

Then deepen each character that matters using the section below.

## Building one character (inner architecture)

Lead with this accessible core. Ask conversationally; don't interrogate.

**1. Want vs. need — the central contradiction.** The engine of a multidimensional
character is that the conscious **want** contradicts the subconscious **need**.

- **Want (external, conscious):** the concrete, plot-level goal they're chasing.
- **Need (internal, subconscious):** what they actually have to learn or heal —
  usually the opposite of what they think they want. (Lester in *American Beauty*
  wants to be young again; he needs to grow up and become truly powerful.)

Push for the gap between them. If want and need are the same thing, the character
is one-dimensional — dig for the contradiction.

**2. Why — the motive.** Audiences don't bond with the want; they bond with the
*why* behind it. Where does this desire come from?

**3. The ghost (backstory wound).** Often the why traces to a past trauma or
formative event — the "ghost." It created a lack the character is always trying to
fill or avoid, usually through unhealthy coping. The best flaws grow from
something that happened to them.

**4. The lie (misbelief).** The ghost tends to leave a false belief the character
lives by — often "I'm only valuable if [condition]" (Woody: if I'm Andy's favorite;
Shrek: I don't deserve love because I'm an ogre). The lie is the source of the
flaw and often of a fear or an obsession. Part of the story is the character
confronting it.

**5. Flaw, fear, secret.** Round the character out:
- **Flaw** — the weakness that makes them make bad choices (connected to the lie).
- **Fear** — the flipside of the want; what they most *don't* want. Fear raises stakes.
- **Secret** — what they hide; it creates inner conflict, and what people are
  ashamed of usually makes the best story.

**6. Method & obstacle & stakes** (the practical scaffolding):
- **Method** — how they pursue the want (cunning, force, charm, knowledge…).
- **Obstacle** — who/what stops them (this is where the opponent connects).
- **Stakes** — what they lose if they fail. No stakes, no interest.

**7. Talents + a memorable physical detail.** Give them a genuine competence
(usually tied to a positive trait), and *one* sticky physical particular — a scar,
a crooked tooth, an odd way of walking — not a head-to-toe catalogue. Test: could
someone pick them out at an airport from your description?

### Optional deeper layer (offer, don't impose)

If the writer wants more rigor, introduce the fuller vocabulary:
- **Spine** — the dominant, often unconscious drive to fill the void; the "itch
  they can't scratch" that powers every action.
- **Comfort concept & façade** — the security blanket the character's identity
  leans on, and the false front built around it to avoid confronting the void.
- **Individuation** — the arc of learning their worth isn't defined by that
  comfort concept / status / role / past (Mr. Incredible beyond "superhero").
- **Worldview** — a generalized belief about how the world works (vs. the lie,
  which is personal), often placing them on one side of the theme's opposition.
- **Obsession** — a built-in hunger; drive the story either by chasing it or by
  taking it away (deprival super-reaction — Woody loses top-toy status).

## Discipline (enforce throughout — this is what makes the skill worth using)

**§1 — No hero in a vacuum.** The biggest character mistake is treating the hero as
an isolated individual; it yields a weak hero and cardboard everyone-else. Keep
connecting each character to the others by contrast. If the writer only has a
protagonist, push (at least lightly) toward the opponent — defining the opponent is
often the key to defining the hero.

**§2 — No one is 100% anything.** A flawless protagonist is a Mary-Sue; a
motiveless evil antagonist is Snidely Whiplash. Mix good and bad in both. The
antagonist should be the hero's **dark parallel** — the protagonist of their own
story, who believes they're right (Holmes/Moriarty, Batman/Joker, Luke/Vader). Ask
what the hero must *learn*, and shape the opponent to embody its opposite.

**§3 — Character is choice under pressure.** Characterization (age, job, dress,
mannerisms) is the surface; *true character* is revealed by the choices a person
makes under pressure — the greater the pressure, the deeper the revelation. A
choice with nothing at risk reveals nothing. When the writer describes who a
character "is," turn it into: *what choice, under what pressure, would prove it?*
Structure and character are the same thing — if you change the choice, you change
the character.

**§4 — Show, don't state; the iceberg.** Readers bond with a character by
*observing* them act, react, and interact and drawing their own conclusions — not by
being told "she was studious." Know as much as possible about the character
(their whole story), but only the tip shows; the rest feeds behavior in subtle
ways. Convert exposition into action, gesture, and choice. Physical description,
too, is shown (a character stretches for a high shelf → we learn they're short).

**§5 — Relationships are arcs, not states.** Every important relationship should
*change* over the story — a flat relationship is a missed subplot. Conflict (even
small awkwardness, or negotiation over an external problem) is what forms and
reveals bonds; as characters clash they alter each other and spin out into new
configurations. For each key pair, ask about the beginning, middle, and end.

**§6 — Dialogue carries subtext.** Speech should sound true and writhe with
meaning: it can leak who they are, what they want, their status, background, and
the gap between their true self and the front they present. Favor lines dense with
subtext over on-the-nose statement.

## Closing out — suggest a character-profile structure

When a character (or cast) has real shape, offer (don't impose) a way to organize
it. Suggested layout — adapt to their scale:

For a single character:

```
character-<name>.md
  # Surface (characterization): role/job, age, one memorable physical detail
  # Want (conscious, external goal)  ·  Need (subconscious, what they must learn)
  # Why / motive
  # Ghost (backstory wound)  ·  Lie / misbelief
  # Flaw  ·  Fear  ·  Secret
  # Method · Obstacle · Stakes
  # Talents
  # Key choice-under-pressure that reveals true character
  # (optional) spine · comfort concept · façade · worldview · arc/individuation
```

For a cast:

```
cast/
  00-web.md          # roles by contrast to the hero; the central hero–opponent conflict
  hero.md
  opponent.md        # note the dark-parallel relationship to the hero
  ally.md
  subplot-<name>.md  # the parallel problem, resolved differently
  relationships.md   # each key pair's arc: beginning · middle · end
```

Guidance to pass on:
- Lead each profile with **want vs. need** — every other line serves that contradiction.
- Keep the profile deep but remember the **iceberg**: most of it never appears on
  the page directly; it surfaces through choices, gestures, and dialogue.
- The profile is done when it's serving the story, not when every field is filled.
