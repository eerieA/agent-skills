Review a writer's existing characters for the things that make characters feel
flat, generic, or unconvincing. This is the critique counterpart to
`/character-building`. Do not rewrite their characters or impose your taste — find
concrete problems and propose targeted fixes.

## Intake

Ask the writer to provide their character material (profiles, notes, or a draft)
and, if not obvious, the **premise/logline** and the story's **theme**. You can't
judge whether a want/need or an opponent serves the story without knowing the
story. If they give a draft rather than notes, audit the characters *as they appear
on the page*, which also lets you judge delivery (categories 5–7 below).

## Audit checklist

Work through each category. For each finding, record:
- **Location** — file:line, section name, character name, or a short quote
- **Severity** — High / Medium / Low (see definitions)
- **Category** — which one below
- **What goes wrong** — the concrete failure, not just the label
- **Suggested fix** — one line

Present findings as a markdown table grouped by severity. After the table, ask
which findings to address before changing anything.

---

### 1. Want without need (High)

A character has a conscious goal but no contradicting subconscious need, so they
read as one-dimensional and can't carry an arc.

Look for:
- A stated want with no deeper need, or a need identical to the want
- No motive ("why") behind the want — audiences bond with the why, not the goal
- No ghost/wound or lie that explains the behavior

**Fix pattern:** find the subconscious need that contradicts the want (what they
must learn/heal, often the opposite of what they chase), and the ghost + lie that
produced it. The gap between want and need is where the character comes alive.

---

### 2. Flat / too-perfect / too-evil (High)

Characters who are 100% one thing. A flawless protagonist (Mary-Sue) or a
motiveless-evil antagonist (Snidely Whiplash) breaks believability.

Look for:
- A protagonist with no real flaw, fear, or secret
- An antagonist who is evil for its own sake, with no understandable motive
- No inconsistency, contradiction, or surprising human behavior anywhere

**Fix pattern:** mix good and bad into both. Give the hero a flaw (ideally rooted
in a ghost), a fear, and a secret. Give the antagonist a justification they'd
defend — see category 3.

---

### 3. Opponent as obstacle, not dark parallel (High)

The antagonist merely blocks the hero mechanically, instead of wanting the same
thing and embodying the theme's opposite.

Look for:
- An opponent who doesn't want the same deep thing the hero wants
- No thematic or personal mirror between hero and opponent
- The hero defined in isolation, with cardboard opposition around them

**Fix pattern:** make the opponent the hero's shadow — the protagonist of their own
story who believes they're right (Holmes/Moriarty, Luke/Vader). Anchor the deepest
conflict they share, and let it embody the opposite of what the hero must learn.

---

### 4. Weak or missing cast web (Medium)

Secondary characters aren't defined by contrast to the hero, so they're thin and
the hero is under-defined too.

Look for:
- Allies/subplot characters with no distinguishing function or contrast
- A subplot character who doesn't face a parallel problem resolved differently
- Relationships that stay static — a flat relationship is a missed subplot

**Fix pattern:** define each character by how they contrast with the hero; give the
subplot character a parallel dilemma with a different outcome; give each key
relationship an arc (beginning → middle → end) driven by conflict or collaboration.

---

### 5. Characterization dumped, not shown (Medium — draft only)

Who a character is gets *stated* in exposition instead of revealed through action,
so readers can't observe and bond. Applies to prose, not private notes.

Look for:
- Narrator statements of trait ("she was hardworking and kind") not dramatized
- Backstory delivered as a block rather than surfacing through behavior
- No scene of the character *choosing* under pressure to prove who they are

**Fix pattern:** convert stated traits into action, gesture, and choice — especially
a choice under pressure (the greater the risk, the truer the revealed character).
Let readers draw the conclusion.

---

### 6. Forgettable physical description (Low–Medium — draft only)

Either a head-to-toe catalogue (a form of telling) or no memorable hook at all.

Look for:
- Exhaustive feature-by-feature descriptions that read like an infodump
- Characters with no distinguishing physical particular
- Traits told once and never made to matter in how the character moves

**Fix pattern:** cut to one or two sticky details (a scar, a crooked tooth, an odd
gait) and show them in motion. Test: could someone pick this character out at an
airport from the description?

---

### 7. Dialogue without subtext (Medium — draft only)

Speech that only conveys surface information — on-the-nose, interchangeable between
characters, no personality or hidden agenda.

Look for:
- Lines that state feelings/intentions directly with no tension beneath
- Dialogue that would sound the same in any character's mouth
- No gap between what a character says and what they want or hide

**Fix pattern:** compress and layer — let lines leak status, background, values, and
the tension between the true self and the presented front. Give each voice a
distinct texture.

---

## Severity definitions

| Severity | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| High | Breaks believability or kills the arc (no need, flat/too-perfect, obstacle-only opponent) |
| Medium | Weakens depth or immersion; fixable without reinventing the character |
| Low | Minor polish; missed opportunity, no real harm |

---

## After presenting findings

1. Show the findings table, grouped by severity.
2. Ask: "Which findings should I work on? (e.g. 'all High', 'items 1 3 5', 'skip the draft-only ones')"
3. Address only what the writer approves — one finding at a time, showing the
   concrete before/after or suggestion.
4. Don't reinvent their characters, rename them, or add backstory they didn't ask
   for. Keep the characters theirs.
