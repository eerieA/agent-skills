# Sources

Personal notes on what this skill was built from. Not copied when the skill is
imported into a project — this is a memory-jog for the author, not part of the skill.

The skill distills these sources; it does not reproduce them. Follow the links /
titles below to the originals.

| Source | What it contributed |
|---|---|
| Michelle Barker, "Building a Fictional World: A Novelist's Guide" — darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/world-building | The 8 world-building categories (geography, government, economy, daily life, religion, history, language, tech/magic); the "don't over-build / rabbit hole" warning; **"the world is not the story"**; one-new-thing-at-a-time and familiar landmarks (the direwolf example). |
| Chris Andrews, *Character and Structure: The Foundations of Fiction* (ISBN 978-1925803068) | The core spine: **rules generate conflict**; keep to ≤10 rules; make limits/costs explicit; Asimov's Laws, Road Runner, Buffy, *Avatar* as worked examples. |
| Ross Hartmann, *The Structure of Story* (ISBN 978-1735603827) | The **metaphor method** ("what is X in the world of Y?" → Hogwarts, TIE fighters); the research method — hunt beings, occupations, significant objects, value systems; pursue what evokes awe. |
| Will Storr, *The Science of Storytelling* (ISBN 978-1419743030) | Why **specific sensory detail** makes a world feel real (~3 concrete qualities per object); show-don't-tell as a model-building necessity; filmic word order. |
| Janet Burroway, *Writing Fiction: A Guide to Narrative Craft* (ISBN 978-0226616698) | **Setting carries emotion**; harmony vs. conflict between character and setting; summary vs. scene (delivery, background context). |

This skill leans hardest on Andrews (rules) and Barker (categories + discipline),
with Hartmann for generative method and Storr/Burroway for how the world reaches
the page.
