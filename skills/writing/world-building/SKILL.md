Guide the writer through building a fiction world by interview, not by dumping a
questionnaire on them. Ask a few questions at a time, react to their answers, and
push back when the world starts to violate the discipline principles below. The
goal is a world that feels real and *generates story* — not an encyclopedia.

Work in the conversation. Do not create files unless the writer asks. When the
world has enough shape, offer the world-bible structure at the end (see "Closing
out") so they can save it however they like.

---

## First: orient the world (do this before any category questions)

Ask for whatever of these you don't already have. Two or three questions at a
time — this is a conversation.

1. **Premise / logline.** What story (or kind of story) will live in this world?
   Everything below serves this. If they don't have one yet, that's fine — but
   flag that world-building without a story to serve is the #1 procrastination
   trap (see Discipline §3).
2. **Genre.** Medieval fantasy, sci-fi, contemporary, historical, horror, etc.
   Detect it from their premise if they don't say. This selects the lens
   (see "Genre lenses" below).
3. **The one-line seed.** Push for the single image, "what if," or metaphor at
   the heart of it. Often the strongest worlds come from a **metaphor**: "What is
   X in the world of Y?" — school in the world of wizards is Hogwarts; dogfighting
   in the world of space is TIE-fighter battles. If they have a flat premise, try
   generating two or three metaphor angles to find the one that sparks.

Prioritize what evokes awe or fascination. The seed's job is to make both of you
lean in.

---

## The world rules (do this early — it's the spine)

Before geography or economy, establish **3 to 10 hard rules** for the world. This
is the most important and most-skipped step, so do not skip it.

A rule is anything you promise the audience is always true. Rules matter because
they *generate conflict* — they set boundaries, define relationships, and create
the problems characters can't easily solve.

- Humans can't breathe underwater → crossing the ocean is now a problem.
- Magic always comes at a cost → no free wins.
- An avatar can only be piloted by a genetically compatible person (*Avatar*) →
  forces an unlikely character into the lead.
- A robot may not injure a human (Asimov's First Law) → drives dozens of plots.

Coach the writer to:
- Keep it to **≤10 rules.** More than that and neither of you can track them.
- Prefer rules that **create problems for the protagonist**, not just flavor.
- For each rule, ask: *"What conflict does this generate? Whom does it constrain?"*
  If a proposed rule generates no conflict, it's set-dressing, not a rule — note it
  elsewhere and move on.
- Make the **limits** explicit, especially for magic/tech. "What can't it do?
  Under what conditions does it fail? What does it cost?" A power with no limit
  resolves the story in sentence one.
- Bend rules only with strong justification; never break them silently.

Write the rules down (in the conversation) as a short numbered list. They anchor
everything after.

## Category interview (breadth — filter by what the story needs)

Walk these categories, but **only go deep where the premise and rules demand it.**
Tell the writer explicitly that they don't need to answer everything — most of
this never appears on the page (see Discipline §3). Ask the two or three questions
per category that matter most for *their* story.

1. **Geography & environment** — terrain, climate, seasons. Cross-check for
   coherence: weather must match the crops, the crops must match what people eat,
   the landscape must match how people travel.
2. **Government & politics** — who holds power, how it's kept, what the legal
   system is. Must fit the history.
3. **Economy & trade** — what's scarce, what's valued, who has money and why.
4. **Daily life & customs** — food, clothing, work, family, manners. This is where
   sensory detail comes from later, so mine it.
5. **Religion & belief** — what people worship, fear, or explain the world with.
6. **History & lore** — the past that produced the present. Keep it need-to-know;
   history should explain today's tensions, not become a timeline for its own sake.
7. **Language & communication** — naming conventions, how word travels, literacy.
8. **Technology or magic** — the system, and (critically) its limits and costs.
   This overlaps heavily with the rules above.

Also, per the story-world research method, actively hunt for:
- **Categories of beings** and **occupations** in this world (what does a detective,
  a chef, a priest look like *here*?).
- **Significant objects** — things characters manipulate; anything revered or
  symbolic.
- **Value systems** — what this culture prizes, fears, shames, forbids. These
  convert directly into character desires and fears.

Coherence is the through-line: history → government → economy → daily life should
form one causal chain, not five unrelated lists. Whenever an answer contradicts an
earlier one, surface it and help them reconcile.

## Genre lenses

Apply the matching lens over the core. The core is identical; the lens changes
*which questions bite hardest* and *what fails*.

- **Medieval fantasy** — Magic system is the high-risk area: nail its rules,
  limits, and cost first. Watch for anachronism (potatoes, glass windows, plate
  armor in the wrong century) and for magic that trivializes conflict. Ask how
  magic reshapes economy and power, not just combat.
- **Sci-fi** — The tech and its plausibility is the high-risk area. Establish
  what physics you're keeping and what you're breaking, and be consistent (FTL,
  AI, biotech). Ask the social second-order effects — "who does this technology
  put out of work / give power to?" — which is where the story usually lives.
- **Contemporary / realistic** — The risk is research accuracy and cliché. The
  reader knows this world, so errors break trust instantly. Push for the specific,
  lived-in, non-touristy detail of the real place/subculture; avoid the generic
  postcard version.
- **Historical** — As contemporary, plus period accuracy: material culture,
  mores, what people *couldn't* know or say. Distinguish what's documented from
  what you're inventing in the gaps.
- **Horror / other** — Establish the rules of the threat (what it can/can't do,
  what wards it off) — an unbounded threat isn't scary, it's arbitrary.

If unsure of genre, ask. Don't assume fantasy.

## Discipline (enforce these throughout — this is what makes the skill worth using)

**§1 — Rules generate conflict.** (See "World rules.") A world with no hard rules
produces no problems and therefore no story. Keep steering back to conflict.

**§2 — The world is not the story.** The world is the backdrop; it will constantly
try to become the story. Don't let it. If the writer is elaborating the world past
what any story needs, name it and redirect to the premise. A brilliant world with
no story doesn't sell.

**§3 — Don't over-build (the rabbit hole).** Elaborate systems are seductive
procrastination — easier than writing. Build only what the story needs plus a thin
margin. If a reader would need a flow chart to follow it, it's too complex.
Simplicity wins.

**§4 — Show, don't tell; filter it in.** Almost none of this world-building appears
directly on the page, and none of it as an infodump. It surfaces indirectly — a
character short of breath in a corset, a goat in a field. When we discuss how a
piece of the world reaches the reader, favor **specific sensory detail** (a study
found ~3 concrete qualities — "a dark blue carpet," "an orange striped pencil" —
is what makes a scene vivid) over abstract statement.

**§5 — One new thing at a time; give landmarks.** The reader arrives with fresh
eyes. Introduce the strange against a backdrop of the familiar (Martin's direwolf
is understood via the pony and the kennel dogs the reader already knows). Don't
bombard. And if an invented creature has four legs, a tail, and barks — call it
a dog. No weirdness for its own sake.

**§6 — Setting carries emotion.** Setting is never neutral backdrop; it reflects or
resists the viewpoint character's mood. When the writer describes a place, ask
whose eyes we see it through and what that harmony or friction is doing — that
friction is often where the story starts.

## Closing out — suggest a world-bible structure

When the world has real shape, offer (don't impose) a way to organize it into
files. Suggested layout — adapt to their scale:

```
world-bible/
  00-premise-and-rules.md   # logline, seed/metaphor, the 3–10 hard rules
  01-geography.md
  02-society.md             # government, economy, daily life, customs
  03-belief-and-history.md
  04-magic-or-tech.md       # system + explicit limits & costs
  05-beings-and-factions.md
  06-key-objects.md
  07-language-and-names.md
  08-open-questions.md       # deliberately unresolved; fill on a need-to-know basis
```

Guidance to pass on:
- Lead the bible with **premise + rules** — the two things every other page serves.
- Keep an **open-questions** page. Not everything needs an answer now; resolve
  details when a scene actually demands them.
- Tag entries by whether they'll likely reach the page or are just author
  scaffolding. Most is scaffolding, and that's correct.

Then remind them, once: go write the story. The bible is done when it's serving
the story, not when it's "complete."
